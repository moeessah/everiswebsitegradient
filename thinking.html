Points of view

How we thinkabout owningsoftware.

Not a list of values. Not a mission statement. The actual beliefs that shape every decision we make when we buy and run a product.

scroll

01

Software earns trust slowly.

The reputation that makes a product worth buying took years to build. Every pricing change, every support response, every feature decision accumulated into something customers rely on. A new owner who does not understand that will unravel it faster than they could possibly measure.

The first instinct of a good buyer is to listen, not to change. There is always time to make things better. There is no recovering trust that gets broken in the first six months.

02

A product's character is not an accident.

The personality of a great product came from real decisions made by real people who cared. That is not decoration. It is the product. When new owners strip that out to put their own stamp on things, they are not improving the business. They are consuming it.

Everis tries to do the opposite: understand what made the character, protect it, and build on top of it.

03

AI should be quiet about itself.

The best AI in a product is the kind you forget is there. True enhancement means making something faster, smarter, or easier, then letting it work quietly. Shoehorning AI into a product just to label it 'AI' doesn't help.

We look for places where the tool genuinely helps the user do the thing they came to do. That is a much smaller list than people generally claim, but it is a real one.

04

Long horizons change what you optimize for.

If you plan to own something for a decade, you make different decisions than if you plan to sell it in three years. You care more about whether customers are genuinely happy and less about whether the monthly growth rate looks good on a deck.

You invest in things that compound slowly: support quality, product reliability, team culture. That is the kind of ownership we are building. Less exciting to describe. More durable in practice.

05

We have done this before.

Time Doctor ran for over a decade before Everis existed. Running Remote went from an idea to a real, recurring thing. None of this is theory. The beliefs above came from years of decisions, some good, some that took time to recover from.

When Rob and Liam talk about what it costs to build something worth keeping, they are describing what they watched happen inside their own businesses, over real time, with real consequences.

THINK

In practice

What these beliefs look like
when a deal is on the table.

On diligence

Direct questions. No manufactured urgency.

We ask what we need to know. We say what we think. If the fit is not real, we say so clearly and early.

On the transition

A handoff should feel like a handoff.

Not a drop-off. The team, the customers, and the product all need a transition that makes sense and we plan for it.

On product decisions

Change what needs changing. Protect what does not.

The hardest thing in a new acquisition is knowing the difference. We try to sit with the product for a while before we decide.
